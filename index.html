Section 01: Overview
What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering attack where criminals impersonate legitimate organizations or individuals to trick victims into revealing sensitive information — such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

The term comes from "fishing" — attackers cast a wide net hoping victims will "bite." The attack typically arrives via email, SMS, phone call, or a fake website designed to look like a trusted source.

Section 02: Types
Types of Phishing Attacks
✉
Email Phishing
Mass emails impersonating banks, social media, or services. Most common type.
🎯
Spear Phishing
Targeted attack on a specific person using personalized information to appear credible.
📱
Smishing
Phishing via SMS text messages. Often fake delivery notifications or bank alerts.
📞
Vishing
Voice phishing via phone calls. Attackers pose as bank agents or tech support.
🐋
Whaling
Targets high-value individuals like CEOs or executives. Highly sophisticated.
🔗
Clone Phishing
A legitimate email is cloned and resent with malicious links or attachments.
Section 03: Recognition
10 Red Flags to Spot
01
Suspicious sender address
The email looks like it's from a known company but the domain is slightly off — e.g. "support@paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com".
02
Urgent or threatening language
"Your account will be closed in 24 hours!" — urgency is used to pressure you into acting without thinking.
03
Generic greetings
"Dear Customer" or "Dear User" instead of your actual name — a sign the email was sent in bulk.
04
Suspicious links
Hover over links before clicking. The URL shown may say "amazon.com" but actually leads to "amaz0n-secure.ru".
05
Unexpected attachments
Attachments ending in .exe, .zip, .docm, or even .pdf can carry malware. Never open unexpected files.
06
Poor grammar and spelling
While attackers are getting better, many phishing emails still contain obvious typos and awkward phrasing.
07
Requests for sensitive information
Legitimate companies will NEVER ask for your password, full credit card number, or PIN via email.
08
Too good to be true offers
"You've won a prize!" or "Claim your free iPhone now!" — if it sounds too good, it almost certainly is.
09
Mismatched branding
Logos look slightly off, colors don't match, fonts are wrong. Attackers often slightly botch the visual design.
10
No HTTPS on the website
If a website asks for login credentials but shows "http://" (not "https://"), leave immediately.
Section 04: Case Studies
Real-World Examples
Case Study 01
The Fake PayPal Email

Millions of users received emails claiming their PayPal account was "limited." The email used PayPal's real logo and colors. A link directed victims to a fake login page that harvested credentials.

The red flags: the sender was "noreply@paypal-secure-alerts.com", the link went to a non-PayPal domain, and the email had a sense of urgency.

Lesson: Always check the sender domain and hover over links before clicking.
Case Study 02
Google & Facebook ($100M)

Between 2013–2015, Evaldas Rimasauskas sent fake invoices to Google and Facebook impersonating a real Taiwanese hardware vendor. Employees wired over $100 million before the fraud was detected.

This was a Business Email Compromise (BEC) attack — a sophisticated form of spear phishing targeting finance departments.

Lesson: Always verify wire transfer requests through a second channel (phone call).
Case Study 03
Pakistan Bank SMS Scam

Bank customers received SMS messages claiming their accounts were blocked due to suspicious activity. A phone number was provided. When called, attackers posing as bank agents extracted OTP codes and drained accounts.

This is a classic smishing + vishing combo attack targeting mobile banking users.

Lesson: Banks never ask for OTPs over SMS or phone. Hang up and call the official number.
Section 05: Defense
How to Stay Safe
Tip 01
Enable Two-Factor Authentication
Even if attackers steal your password, 2FA prevents them from accessing your account without the second factor.
Tip 02
Verify Before You Click
Hover over every link. If the URL doesn't match the expected domain, do not click. Go directly to the official website.
Tip 03
Call Back to Verify
If someone calls claiming to be from your bank or company, hang up and call back using the official number from their website.
Tip 04
Use a Password Manager
Password managers auto-fill credentials only on the correct domain — they won't fill on fake sites, alerting you to fakes.
Tip 05
Report Phishing Attempts
Forward phishing emails to your IT team or report@phishing.gov. This helps authorities track and shut down attackers.
Tip 06
Keep Software Updated
Outdated browsers and email clients are easier to exploit. Enable automatic updates on all your devices.
